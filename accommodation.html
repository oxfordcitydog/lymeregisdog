Staying in Lyme Regis with a dog

Lyme Regis is well set up for dogs, and there is a lot of choice: self-catering cottages and apartments in the town, beach chalets along the front, bed and breakfasts, pubs with rooms, and campsites and caravan parks in the surrounding countryside. Three of the four beaches welcome dogs all year round, most cafés and independent shops are relaxed about them, and the coast path runs straight through — so once you are here, having a dog with you rarely limits what you can do.

The thing worth thinking hardest about is not whether a place takes dogs, but where in the town it sits. Lyme Regis is built down the side of a very steep hill.

Where to stay, and what the hill means

Down near the seafront

Easiest with a dog · beaches on the doorstep

Staying low means beach walks without a climb at either end, which matters enormously if your dog is elderly, arthritic, or simply small. You are also within a flat stroll of Marine Parade's cafés. The trade-offs are noise in high summer, tighter parking, and generally higher prices.

Up in the town and above it

Better value and views · a genuine climb home

Properties up Broad Street and above have the views, the parking and often the better prices. But every walk to the beach ends with the hill, and after a long day on the coast path that climb is real. Fine for a fit dog, hard work for an old one.

The Uplyme and Lim Valley side

Flat walking from the door · quieter

Staying towards Uplyme puts you on the flat Lim Valley path, the gentlest walking anywhere near the town, with the river alongside. Quieter, greener, and a short drive or level walk into Lyme itself. Our pick for anyone travelling with a dog who cannot manage hills.

Campsites and caravan parks nearby

Usually the most dog-relaxed option

The countryside around Lyme Regis, Charmouth and Uplyme has campsites and caravan parks, and these are often the easiest places of all with a dog — outdoor space, fewer rules about muddy paws, and frequently no dog charge at all. Check whether dogs may be left in a caravan or awning, as many sites say no, and expect livestock in neighbouring fields.

What to ask before you book

Most disappointments come from things that were technically in the terms and conditions. Worth asking directly, by email, so you have it in writing:

  • How many dogs, and is there a charge? Usually per dog, per stay, and often buried in the small print rather than the headline listing.
  • Can the dog be left alone in the property? A great many holiday lets say no, even for an hour. This one catches people out constantly — plan around it if you want to eat somewhere that does not admit dogs.
  • Which rooms are dogs allowed in? Some places mean "downstairs only" or "not on the soft furnishings", which is fine if you know in advance and awkward if you do not.
  • Is there enclosed outdoor space? A secure garden is worth a great deal, especially with a young or bolting dog.
  • Where is the parking, and how far is it from the door? On this hill, a property described as "a short walk from the beach" may mean a short walk down and a long climb up.
  • Is there anywhere to wash a wet, sandy dog? An outside tap, a boot room or a hosepipe transforms a week by the sea.

Arriving without a car

It is perfectly possible. Axminster is the nearest railway station, roughly five miles away, with the X51 and X53 bus services connecting Lyme Regis to Axminster, Bridport and Dorchester. Dogs generally travel free on local buses at the driver's discretion, and on trains under the usual national rules. Timetables are on Traveline South West. Parking information for those who do drive is on the Dorset Council parking pages, and general visitor information from Lyme Regis Town Council.

Common questions

Is there much dog-friendly accommodation in Lyme Regis?

Plenty — cottages, apartments, beach chalets, B&Bs, pubs with rooms, campsites and caravan parks. Most self-catering places take one or two dogs. The map above shows where people have actually stayed and what they thought.

What will I be charged for the dog?

It varies a lot. Self-catering commonly charges a fee per dog per stay; many campsites charge nothing. Always confirm before booking rather than on arrival.

Can I leave my dog in the property while I go out?

Often not. Many holiday lets prohibit leaving dogs unattended at all. Check first — it shapes how you plan evenings, since not everywhere in town admits dogs.

Which part of town is best with an older dog?

Near the seafront, or over towards Uplyme and the Lim Valley where the walking is flat. Avoid anywhere high above the town unless you are happy doing that climb several times a day.

Are the beaches dog-friendly all year?

Three of the four are. Front Beach, the sandy one in the middle of town, is closed to dogs from 1 May to 30 September. Full details on our beaches page.
